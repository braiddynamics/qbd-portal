## 12.4 Formal Synthesis {#12.4}

:::note[**End of Chapter 12**]
:::

We have established the **Microscopic Field Equations** governing the equilibrium state of the causal graph, successfully deriving the discrete analogue of General Relativity directly from the graph's variational and combinatorial properties.

By applying the principles of optimal transport and discrete calculus to the causal substrate, we have demonstrated:
1.  **Local Conservation (12.1):** The condition of detailed balance at the homeostatic fixed point mandates that the net divergence of the complexity flux vanishes. This establishes the discrete stress-energy tensor $T_{ab}$ as a strictly conserved source term, representing the active flow of geometric updates.
2.  **Stationary Action (12.2):** We proved that the thermodynamic equilibrium dictated by the master equation is mathematically isomorphic to the principle of stationary action ($\delta \mathcal{S} = 0$). This yields the discrete Einstein Field Equations $\mathcal{G}_{ab} = \kappa T_{ab}$, demonstrating that spatial curvature emerges naturally as the graph's elastic response to balance the "pressure" of informational flux.
3.  **Discrete General Covariance (12.3):** By translating continuous diffeomorphism invariance into discrete vertex relabeling invariance, we invoked Noether's principle to derive the Discrete Bianchi Identity ($\nabla \cdot \mathcal{G} = 0$). This guarantees the geometric self-consistency of the graph without ad hoc restrictions, proving that the theory respects the relativity of the observer at the fundamental level.
4.  **Universal Coupling (12.2.5):** We identified the gravitational constant $\kappa$ not as an arbitrary free parameter, but as a structural ratio determined by the microscopic discreteness scale $\ell_0$ and the macroscopic correlation length $\xi$, bridging the quantum and cosmic scales.

This chapter has provided the *local* dynamical rules—the "micro-physics" of emergent gravity. It proves that gravity is not a foreign force added to the universe, but the inevitable geometric consequence of the graph maintaining its own computational equilibrium. With these local laws firmly established, we are prepared to take the Gromov-Hausdorff-Wasserstein limit in **Chapter 13**, transitioning from the discrete network to the smooth Riemannian manifold.
